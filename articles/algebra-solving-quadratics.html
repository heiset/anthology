Solving Quadratics

radicals, graphing, square roots, completing the square, and the quadratic formula

\[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\]

The discriminant \(b^2-4ac\): positive → two real roots · zero → one repeated root · negative → no real roots

Simplifying Radicals

Pull out perfect-square factors to simplify a square root. To rationalize a denominator, multiply by the radical (or its conjugate) over itself.

Simplify \(\sqrt{54}\).

\[\sqrt{54} = \sqrt{9 \cdot 6} = 3\sqrt{6}\]

Simplify \(\sqrt{\dfrac{29}{100}}\).

\[\sqrt{\frac{29}{100}} = \frac{\sqrt{29}}{\sqrt{100}} = \frac{\sqrt{29}}{10}\]

Rationalize \(\dfrac{2}{\sqrt{7y}}\).

\[\frac{2}{\sqrt{7y}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{7y}}{\sqrt{7y}} = \frac{2\sqrt{7y}}{7y}\]

Rationalize \(\dfrac{5}{7-\sqrt{2}}\).

Multiply by the conjugate \(7+\sqrt{2}\) — the denominator becomes a difference of squares and the radical disappears:

\[ \frac{5}{7-\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{7+\sqrt{2}}{7+\sqrt{2}} = \frac{5(7+\sqrt{2})}{49 - 2} = \frac{5(7+\sqrt{2})}{47} \]

Graphing Quadratics

For any quadratic in standard form \(y = ax^2+bx+c\), the axis of symmetry is \(x = -\tfrac{b}{2a}\). Substitute that \(x\)-value to find the vertex \(y\)-coordinate. Zeros come from setting \(y=0\) and factoring (or using the formula).

Graph \(-x^2 + 6x = 0\).

\[0 = x(-x+6) \;\Rightarrow\; x = 0 \text{ or } x = 6\] \[x = \frac{-6}{2(-1)} = 3 \qquad y = -(3)^2+6(3) = 9 \qquad \text{vertex }(3,9)\]

Graph \(6x - 7 = -x^2\).

Rearrange: \(y = x^2 + 6x - 7\).

\[0 = (x+7)(x-1) \;\Rightarrow\; x = -7 \text{ or } x = 1\] \[x = \frac{-6}{2(1)} = -3 \qquad y = (-3)^2+6(-3)-7 = -16 \qquad \text{vertex }(-3,-16)\]

Solving with Square Roots

When a quadratic has no linear term (\(b=0\)), isolate \(x^2\) and take the square root of both sides. Remember \(\pm\) — both signs work when squaring.

Solve \(x^2 = 121\).

\[x = \pm\sqrt{121} = \pm 11\]

Solve \(x^2 + 9 = 0\).

\[x^2 = -9 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \pm\sqrt{-9} = \pm 3i\]

No real solutions — the graph of \(y = x^2+9\) sits entirely above the \(x\)-axis with vertex \((0,9)\).

Solve \(5x^2 - 21 = 24\).

\[5x^2 = 45 \;\Rightarrow\; x^2 = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \pm 3\]

Completing the Square

To complete the square, add \(\left(\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2\) to both sides so the left side becomes a perfect square trinomial. Then take the square root and solve.

Solve \(x^2 + 6x = 7\).

\[ \begin{aligned} x^2 + 6x + 9 &= 7 + 9 && \textstyle\left(\frac{6}{2}\right)^2 = 9 \\ (x+3)^2 &= 16 \\ x + 3 &= \pm 4 \\ x &= 1 \text{ or } x = -7 \end{aligned} \]

Practice: Try \(x^2+8x=9\) and \(x^2-10x=11\) with the same method.

For \(x^2+8x=9\): add \(16\) to both sides → \((x+4)^2=25\) → \(x=1\) or \(x=-9\).

For \(x^2-10x=11\): add \(25\) to both sides → \((x-5)^2=36\) → \(x=11\) or \(x=-1\).

Find \(c\) so that \(x^2 - 16x + c\) is a perfect square.

\[c = \left(\frac{-16}{2}\right)^2 = 64 \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad x^2 - 16x + 64 = (x-8)^2\]

Solve \(x^2 + 10x = 16\).

\[ \begin{aligned} x^2 + 10x + 25 &= 41 \\ (x+5)^2 &= 41 \\ x &= -5 \pm \sqrt{41} \end{aligned} \]

Solve \(x^2 - 10x + 17 = 0\).

\[ \begin{aligned} x^2 - 10x &= -17 \\ x^2 - 10x + 25 &= 8 \\ (x-5)^2 &= 8 \\ x &= 5 \pm 2\sqrt{2} \end{aligned} \]

Where the quadratic formula comes from

Every quadratic \(x^2 + bx + c\) (after dividing by the leading coefficient) has two roots \(r\) and \(s\) and a midpoint \(m\) exactly halfway between them. The midpoint is the axis of symmetry.

Since the roots are symmetric about the midpoint: \(r = m - d\) and \(s = m + d\). Expanding \((x-r)(x-s)\):

\[x^2 - (r+s)x + rs = x^2 + bx + c\] \[ \begin{aligned} b &= -(r+s) = -2m && \Rightarrow & m &= -\tfrac{b}{2} \\ c &= rs = (m-d)(m+d) = m^2 - d^2 && \Rightarrow & d &= \sqrt{m^2 - c} \end{aligned} \]

The roots are \(m \pm d\), which is exactly \(-\tfrac{b}{2} \pm \sqrt{\tfrac{b^2}{4} - c}\). For the un-normalized form \(ax^2+bx+c\), divide through by \(a\) first — that's where \(2a\) in the denominator and the \(4ac\) in the discriminant come from.

Example: \(x^2 - 7x + 12 = (x-3)(x-4)\). Midpoint \(m = \tfrac{3+4}{2} = \tfrac{7}{2}\). Distance \(d = \sqrt{m^2 - c} = \sqrt{\tfrac{49}{4} - 12} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{4}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\). Roots: \(\tfrac{7}{2} \pm \tfrac{1}{2}\) — that's 3 and 4. ✓

Harder example: \(x^2 + 6x + 7 = 0\). Midpoint \(m = -3\). Distance \(d = \sqrt{9 - 7} = \sqrt{2}\). Roots: \(-3 \pm \sqrt{2}\), and the factored form is \((x+3+\sqrt{2})(x+3-\sqrt{2})\).

The Quadratic Formula

Solve \(x^2 - 8x + 16 = 0\).

\[ x = \frac{8 \pm \sqrt{64 - 64}}{2} = \frac{8 \pm 0}{2} = 4 \]

The discriminant is zero — one repeated root (the parabola touches the \(x\)-axis at exactly one point).

Solve \(x^2 + 10x - 11 = 0\).

\[ x = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{100 + 44}}{2} = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{144}}{2} = \frac{-10 \pm 12}{2} \] \[x = 1 \quad \text{or} \quad x = -11\]

Systems with a Quadratic

To find where a line and a parabola intersect, set them equal and solve the resulting quadratic. Each solution gives an \(x\)-coordinate; substitute back to find \(y\).

Solve \(y = 4x^2+2x-1\) and \(y = -2x+7\).

\[ \begin{aligned} 4x^2 + 2x - 1 &= -2x + 7 \\ 4x^2 + 4x - 8 &= 0 \\ x^2 + x - 2 &= 0 \\ (x+2)(x-1) &= 0 \\ x &= -2 \text{ or } x = 1 \end{aligned} \] \[y(-2) = -2(-2)+7 = 11 \qquad y(1) = -2(1)+7 = 5\]
\[\boxed{(-2,\,11) \text{ and } (1,\,5)}\]

Solve \(y = 6x\) and \(y = x^2+9\).

\[ \begin{aligned} 6x &= x^2 + 9 \\ 0 &= x^2 - 6x + 9 \\ 0 &= (x-3)^2 \\ x &= 3 \end{aligned} \]

The discriminant is zero — the line is tangent to the parabola, touching at exactly one point.

\[\boxed{(3,\,18)}\]

Solve \(y = x^2-x-1\) and \(y = x-2\).

\[ \begin{aligned} x^2 - x - 1 &= x - 2 \\ x^2 - 2x + 1 &= 0 \\ (x-1)^2 &= 0 \\ x &= 1 \end{aligned} \] \[y = 1 - 2 = -1 \qquad \boxed{(1,\,-1)}\]

Again a tangent — the line just grazes the parabola at one point.
