The Formal Definition of a Limit

epsilon, delta, and the language of precision

Intuition says a limit is the value a function "approaches." The definition makes that precise without using the word "approaches" at all.

We say \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) if, for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that

\[ 0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon. \]

Read it as a game: your opponent picks any tolerance \(\varepsilon\) around \(L\). You must respond with a radius \(\delta\) around \(a\) such that every \(x\) inside that radius (other than \(a\) itself) maps to within \(\varepsilon\) of \(L\). If you can always win, the limit is \(L\).

A worked example

Claim: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3}(2x - 1) = 5\).

We need \(|f(x) - 5| = |2x - 6| = 2|x - 3| < \varepsilon\). Choose \(\delta = \varepsilon / 2\):

\[ 0 < |x - 3| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \implies |2x - 1 - 5| = 2|x-3| < \varepsilon. \]

Why this matters for series

Infinite series are defined in terms of limits: the sum \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k\) is \(\lim_{n \to \infty} S_n\) where \(S_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} a_k\). The epsilon-delta framework becomes the foundation for every convergence theorem.

The definition does not say what \(f(a)\) is, or even that it exists. The limit is about behavior near \(a\), not at \(a\).
