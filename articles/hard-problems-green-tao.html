The Green-Tao Theorem

arithmetic progressions hiding inside the primes

An arithmetic progression is a sequence of numbers equally spaced: \(3, 7, 11, 15, 19\) (spacing 4). Among the primes, short arithmetic progressions appear easily — \(3, 5, 7\) or \(5, 11, 17, 23, 29\). But can we always find longer ones? Is there any limit?

The theorem

Green-Tao Theorem (2004). For any positive integer \(k\), there exist \(k\) primes in arithmetic progression:

\[ p, \quad p + d, \quad p + 2d, \quad \ldots, \quad p + (k-1)d \]

for some prime \(p\) and common difference \(d > 0\).

This is a startling result. Primes thin out as numbers grow larger, and finding long arithmetic progressions would seem to require enormous density — yet the primes manage it despite their sparsity.

The proof strategy

Green and Tao (Tao later won the Fields Medal partly for this work) combined three ingredients:

Szemerédi's theorem: any subset of the integers with positive upper density contains arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions.

A transference principle: the key innovation, extending Szemerédi's result from dense sets to "pseudorandom" sets — sets that locally look random but contain the primes as a substantial portion.

Goldston-Yildirim estimates: showing the primes can be embedded in a pseudorandom set of almost-primes with the right density properties.

The theorem does not give an explicit formula. As of 2019 the longest known arithmetic progression of primes has 27 terms, beginning at a 24-digit number.

Challenge problem

(a) Verify that \(5, 11, 17, 23, 29\) is an arithmetic progression of 5 primes. What is the common difference? Confirm each term is prime by trial division.

(b) Explain why any arithmetic progression of more than 2 primes, all greater than 3, must have an even common difference. Then show the common difference must be divisible by 6 if the progression has more than 3 terms, all greater than 3. (Consider the terms modulo 2 and modulo 3 separately.)

(c) Find a second arithmetic progression of 5 primes, different from the one above, with a common difference that is a multiple of 6.
