Quadratics

roots, axis of symmetry, three forms, and the formula

Every quadratic looks the same — a big U. This gives us nice features to work with. The roots are where the function crosses the \(x\)-axis, also called zeros because they occur where \(y = 0\). The axis of symmetry is in the middle between the roots. The vertex lies on the axis of symmetry at the bottom of the U. Two examples:

\[y = x^2 \qquad\qquad y = (x+3)^2 - 1\]

Three forms of a quadratic

Every quadratic can be written in three equivalent ways. Each form makes different features easy to read off.

1. Factored Form

\[ y = a(x - r)(x - s) \]
  • Roots: \(x = r\) and \(x = s\)
  • Axis of symmetry: \(x = \dfrac{r+s}{2}\)
  • Vertex: plug \(x = \dfrac{r+s}{2}\) into the formula

↓ multiply  |  ↑ factor, or quadratic formula

2. Standard Form

\[ y = ax^2 + bx + c \]
  • Axis of symmetry: \(x = \dfrac{-b}{2a}\)
  • Vertex: plug \(x = \dfrac{-b}{2a}\) into the formula
  • Roots: factor, graph, or use the quadratic formula

↓ complete the square  |  ↑ multiply

3. Vertex Form

\[ y = a(x - h)^2 + k \]
  • Vertex: \((h, k)\)
  • Axis of symmetry: \(x = h\)
  • Roots: set \(y = 0\) and solve for \(x\)

Where the quadratic formula comes from

Start with a general quadratic, divide through by \(a\) to simplify:

\[ ax^2 + bx + c \;\longrightarrow\; x^2 + b'x + c' \]

Any quadratic has two roots \(r\) and \(s\) with a midpoint \(m\) halfway between them, each at distance \(d\) from the middle. Below, \(y = (x-1)(x-3)\) illustrates this with roots at 1 and 3 and midpoint at 2:

In factored form this quadratic is \((x - r)(x - s) = x^2 - (r+s)x + rs\). So:

\[ \begin{aligned} b' &= -(r + s) \\ c' &= rs = (m - d)(m + d) = m^2 - d^2 \end{aligned} \]

The midpoint \(m\) is just \(-b'/2\) (the average of the roots). Once we know \(m\) and \(c'\):

\[ d^2 = m^2 - c' \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad d = \sqrt{m^2 - c'} \]

The roots are \(r = m - d\) and \(s = m + d\). Writing this back in terms of the original \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) gives the quadratic formula:

\[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]

Worked examples

Example 1.   Find the roots of \(x^2 - 7x + 12\).

The midpoint is \(m = -b'/2 = 7/2 = 3\tfrac{1}{2}\). The distance is \(d = \sqrt{m^2 - c'} = \sqrt{12.25 - 12} = \tfrac{1}{2}\). So the roots are \(r_1 = 3\) and \(r_2 = 4\), and the quadratic factors as \((x-3)(x-4)\). Check: \((x-3)(x-4) = x^2 - 7x + 12\).

Example 2.   Find the roots of \(x^2 + 6x + 7 = 0\).

The midpoint is \(m = -6/2 = -3\). The distance is \(d = \sqrt{(-3)^2 - 7} = \sqrt{9 - 7} = \sqrt{2}\). So the roots are \(-3 - \sqrt{2}\) and \(-3 + \sqrt{2}\).

We can also convert to vertex form by completing the square:

\[ \begin{alignat*}{2} x^2 + 6x + 7 &= 0 \quad && \text{start} \\ x^2 + 6x + 9 - 9 + 7 &= 0 && \text{add and subtract } 9 \\ (x + 3)^2 - 2 &= 0 && \text{factor the perfect square} \end{alignat*} \]

This is vertex form \(y = (x - h)^2 + k\) with \(h = -3\), \(k = -2\): vertex at \((-3,\,-2)\), axis of symmetry \(x = -3\).
