Polynomials, Their Derivatives, and Their Roots

A sample proof-style page

Suppose a degree-\(m\) polynomial has distinct roots \(r_1,\dots,r_m\). Then

\[ p(x)=\prod_{k=1}^{m}(x-r_k). \]

Differentiating a product of many factors gives

\[ p'(x) = \sum_{j=1}^{m} \prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^{m}(x-r_k). \]
If \(p\) has \(m\) distinct zeros, then \(p\) and \(p'\) have no zeros in common.

Evaluate the derivative at one of the roots, say \(x=r_j\). Every summand except one contains the factor \(r_j-r_j=0\). The surviving term is

\[ p'(r_j) = \prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\ne j}}^{m}(r_j-r_k). \]

Since the roots are distinct, each factor is nonzero. Therefore \(p'(r_j)\ne0\), so no root of \(p\) is also a root of \(p'\).
