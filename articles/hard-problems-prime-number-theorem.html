The Prime Number Theorem

order hidden inside the chaos of the primes

In 1977 Don Zagier opened a celebrated essay with one of the most striking observations in number theory: primes grow like weeds among the integers, obeying no law but chance — and yet they exhibit stunning regularity, obeying laws of almost military precision. This double nature, local chaos and global order, is the central mystery of the primes.

The theorem

Let \(\pi(n)\) denote the number of primes up to \(n\). The Prime Number Theorem, proved independently by Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin in 1896, states

\[ \pi(n) \sim \frac{n}{\ln n} \]

meaning the ratio \(\pi(n) \,/\, (n / \ln n)\) approaches 1 as \(n \to \infty\). Equivalently, the probability that a random integer near \(n\) is prime is approximately \(1/\ln n\). Near a million, roughly 1 in 14 numbers is prime. Near a trillion, roughly 1 in 28.

A sharper approximation uses the logarithmic integral

\[ \pi(n) \approx \mathrm{Li}(n) = \int_2^n \frac{dt}{\ln t}. \]

The agreement is extraordinary. For \(n = 10^9\) the actual count is \(\pi(10^9) = 50{,}847{,}534\) and \(\mathrm{Li}(10^9) \approx 50{,}849{,}234\) — an error of less than 0.003%. Zagier's "military precision" is not hyperbole.

Challenge problem

(a) Use the approximation \(\pi(n) \approx n / \ln n\) to estimate the number of primes between \(10^6\) and \(2 \times 10^6\). Show your calculation.

(b) The average gap between consecutive primes near \(N\) is approximately \(\ln N\). For \(N = 10^6\), what is this predicted average gap?

(c) Hardy and Littlewood conjectured that the number of twin prime pairs \((p,\, p+2)\) up to \(N\) is approximately

\[ C \cdot \frac{N}{(\ln N)^2}, \quad C \approx 1.3203. \]

Estimate the count of twin prime pairs up to \(10^6\). The actual count is 8,169 — how does your estimate compare?
