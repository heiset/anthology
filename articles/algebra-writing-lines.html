Writing Line Equations

slope-intercept form, parallel and perpendicular lines, data, sequences, and piecewise functions

Slope-Intercept Form

We already know how to graph any line — pick values for \(x\), plug them in, find \(y\). But slope-intercept form \(y = mx + b\) lets us read off the slope and \(y\)-intercept immediately, which opens up faster ways to write and graph lines.

Given slope and intercept directly. Find the line with slope \(3\) and \(y\)-intercept \(-9\):

\[y = 3x - 9\]

From two points. Find the line through \((4, 0)\) and \((0, -7)\):

\[m = \frac{-7 - 0}{0 - 4} = \frac{7}{4}\]

Since \((0, -7)\) already sits on the \(y\)-axis, we can read off \(b = -7\) directly:

\[y = \frac{7}{4}x - 7\]

Using rise over run. Find the line through \((0, 0)\) and \((-2, -3)\):

Going from \((-2,-3)\) to \((0,0)\): the line rises \(3\) and runs \(2\), so \(m = \tfrac{3}{2}\). The origin is on the line, so \(b = 0\):

\[y = \frac{3}{2}x\]

Using function notation. Find the line where \(f(6) = -2\) and \(f(0) = -5\). Translated into points: \((6, -2)\) and \((0, -5)\). Since \(f(0) = -5\), the \(y\)-intercept is \(b = -5\) directly.

\[m = \frac{-2 - (-5)}{6 - 0} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad y = \frac{1}{2}x - 5\]

Line Equations from Two Points

When no graph is given, the process is always the same: find slope first, then substitute one point into \(y = mx + b\) to solve for \(b\).

Point \((-4, 5)\), slope \(m = 1\).

\[ \begin{aligned} y &= mx + b \\ 5 &= (1)(-4) + b && \text{substitute } (-4,5) \text{ and } m=1\\ 5 &= -4 + b \\ 9 &= b \\ y &= x + 9 \end{aligned} \]

Points \((-5, 3)\) and \((-2, 0)\).

\[ \begin{aligned} m &= \frac{0 - 3}{-2 - (-5)} = \frac{-3}{3} = -1 \\[6pt] 3 &= (-1)(-5) + b \implies 3 = 5 + b \implies b = -2 \\[4pt] y &= -x - 2 \end{aligned} \]

Points \((-3, 6)\) and \((-5, -6)\).

\[ \begin{aligned} m &= \frac{-6 - 6}{-5 - (-3)} = \frac{-12}{-2} = 6 \\[6pt] 6 &= 6(-3) + b \implies 6 = -18 + b \implies b = 24 \\[4pt] y &= 6x + 24 \end{aligned} \]

Function notation: \(f(-5) = 2\) and \(f(7) = -4\). Points: \((-5, 2)\) and \((7, -4)\).

\[ \begin{aligned} m &= \frac{-4 - 2}{7 - (-5)} = \frac{-6}{12} = -\frac{1}{2} \\[6pt] 2 &= -\tfrac{1}{2}(-5) + b \implies 2 = \tfrac{5}{2} + b \implies b = -\tfrac{1}{2} \\[4pt] y &= -\tfrac{1}{2}x - \tfrac{1}{2} \end{aligned} \]

From a table. Pick any two clean points — say \((1, 3)\) and \((3, 2)\):

\(x\) 1 3 5 7
\(y\) 3 2 1 0
\[ \begin{aligned} m &= \frac{2 - 3}{3 - 1} = -\frac{1}{2} \\[6pt] 3 &= -\tfrac{1}{2}(1) + b \implies b = \tfrac{7}{2} \\[4pt] y &= -\tfrac{1}{2}x + \tfrac{7}{2} \end{aligned} \]

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Parallel lines have the same slope. Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other — their product is always \(-1\).

Identifying parallel lines. Three lines defined by point pairs:

  • Line a: through \((-1, 4)\) and \((1, 5)\) \(\;\Rightarrow\; m = \dfrac{5-4}{1-(-1)} = \dfrac{1}{2}\)
  • Line b: through \((-2, 7)\) and \((0, 4)\) \(\;\Rightarrow\; m = \dfrac{4-7}{0-(-2)} = -\dfrac{3}{2}\)
  • Line c: through \((0, 4)\) and \((2, 5)\) \(\;\Rightarrow\; m = \dfrac{5-4}{2-0} = \dfrac{1}{2}\)

Lines a and c share slope \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), so \(a \parallel c\).

Writing a parallel line. Find the line through \((14, 3)\) parallel to \(2y - x = 8\).

\[ \begin{aligned} 2y - x &= 8 \implies y = \tfrac{1}{2}x + 4 && \text{identify slope: } m = \tfrac{1}{2} \text{ (discard }b\text{)} \\ 3 &= \tfrac{1}{2}(14) + b && \text{substitute } (14,3) \\ 3 &= 7 + b \implies b = -4 \\ y &= \tfrac{1}{2}x - 4 \end{aligned} \]

Classifying three lines. With points:

  • Line a: \((-5,-2)\) and \((1,-1)\) \(\;\Rightarrow\; m_a = \tfrac{1}{6}\)
  • Line b: \((-3,5)\) and \((3,6)\) \(\;\Rightarrow\; m_b = \tfrac{1}{6}\)
  • Line c: \((0,7)\) and \((1,1)\) \(\;\Rightarrow\; m_c = -6\)

\(a \parallel b\) (same slope). And \(a \perp c\), \(b \perp c\) since \(\tfrac{1}{6} \cdot (-6) = -1\).

Writing a perpendicular line. Find the line through \((-3, 1)\) perpendicular to \(y = -5x + 2\).

The given slope is \(-5\). A perpendicular slope flips the sign and inverts: \(m = \tfrac{1}{5}\).

\[ \begin{aligned} 1 &= \tfrac{1}{5}(-3) + b \implies 1 = -\tfrac{3}{5} + b \implies b = \tfrac{8}{5} \\[4pt] y &= \tfrac{1}{5}x + \tfrac{8}{5} \end{aligned} \]

Scatterplots and Correlation

A scatterplot plots pairs of measurements as individual points to reveal trends. When points trend upward together — as one variable grows, so does the other — that's positive correlation. When one grows as the other shrinks, that's negative correlation. When there's no consistent pattern, there's no correlation.

A scatterplot of basketball players' season scoring average (x-axis) against their tournament scoring average (y-axis) shows points trending upward — players with higher season averages tend to score more in tournaments. That's positive correlation.

If instead the points scatter with no direction — sometimes high, sometimes low as \(x\) increases — there's no correlation. And a scatterplot where points trend downward (say, hours of TV watched vs GPA) is negative correlation.

Line of best fit — roll sales over six weeks.

Week \((x)\) 1 2 3 4 5 6
Rolls sold \((y)\) 6 7 11 15 19 26

Estimate a line of best fit using the first and last points \((1, 6)\) and \((6, 26)\):

\[m = \frac{26 - 6}{6 - 1} = \frac{20}{5} = 4 \qquad 6 = 4(1) + b \implies b = 2 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad y = 4x + 2\]

The slope \(4\) means about 4 more rolls sold per week. The \(y\)-intercept \(2\) estimates roughly 2 rolls already sold before week 1.

Residuals and Model Fit

A residual is the difference between the actual \(y\) and the value the model predicts: \(\text{residual} = y - \hat{y}\). We put a hat on \(\hat{y}\) to distinguish what we calculate from what we observe. Residuals scattered randomly near zero mean a good fit; a clear pattern in the residuals means the model is missing something.

Model \(y = \tfrac{3}{2}x - 10\).

\(x\) 2 4 6 8 10 12 14 16 18
actual \(y\) −1 −1 1 2 5 6 8 10 14
\(\hat{y}\) −7 −4 −1 2 5 8 11 14 17
residual 6 3 2 0 0 −2 −3 −4 −3

The residuals drift steadily from \(+6\) down to \(-3\) — a clear systematic pattern, not random scatter. This model is not a good fit.

Model \(y = -2x + 56\).

\(x\) 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8 9
actual \(y\) 52 50 48 47 45 42 41 38 35
\(\hat{y}\) 54 52 50 48 46 44 42 40 38
residual −2 −2 −2 −1 −1 −2 −1 −2 −3

Residuals are small and consistently around \(-1\) to \(-3\) — no strong trend, but always negative. Reasonable fit, though the model slightly overpredicts. Lowering the \(y\)-intercept by \(1\) or \(2\) would improve it.

Arithmetic Sequences

An arithmetic sequence is a list of numbers where each term adds a fixed amount to the one before. That fixed amount is the common difference \(d\). The counting numbers \(1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots\) are an arithmetic sequence with \(d = +1\). The sequence \(47, 49, 51, 53, \ldots\) has \(d = +2\).

To find the formula for the \(n\)th term:

  1. Find \(d\) by subtracting adjacent terms.
  2. Start with \(a_n = d \cdot n\) and check what it gives for \(n = 1\).
  3. Add a constant to shift the sequence so the first term is correct.

Sequence \(-9,\,-1,\,7,\,15,\,\ldots\)

Each term is \(8\) bigger than the last, so \(d = 8\). Try \(a_n = 8n\): the first term would be \(8 \cdot 1 = 8\), but the real first term is \(-9\). The gap is \(-9 - 8 = -17\), so adjust:

\[a_n = 8n - 17\]
\(n\) 1 2 3 4 50
\(a_n\) −9 −1 7 15 383

Sequence \(-160,\,-180,\,-200,\,-220,\,\ldots\)

Each term decreases by \(20\), so \(d = -20\). Try \(a_n = -20n\): the first term would be \(-20\), but we need \(-160\). The gap is \(-160 - (-20) = -140\), so adjust:

\[a_n = -20n - 140\]

Piecewise Functions

A piecewise function uses different rules on different intervals. Read the condition on the right to know which rule applies. To graph one: identify the intervals, draw a dashed vertical line at each boundary, then graph each piece like a normal line — but only inside its interval.

Two-piece function.

\[f(x) = \begin{cases} -x + 5, & x < 5 \\ x - 5, & x \ge 5 \end{cases}\]

The left branch (\(x < 5\)) goes down to \((5, 0)\); the right branch (\(x \ge 5\)) picks up there and goes back up. The orange dashed line marks where the rule changes.

Three-piece function.

\[f(x) = \begin{cases} -x + 1, & x < -3 \\ 4, & -3 \le x < 0 \\ 3x + 4, & x \ge 0 \end{cases}\]

The left piece slopes downward into \((-3, 4)\); the middle piece holds flat at \(y = 4\) across to \((0, 4)\); the right piece takes off steeply from there. The orange lines divide the graph into the three intervals.
