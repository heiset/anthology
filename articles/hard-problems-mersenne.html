If \(2^n - 1\) Is Prime, So Is \(n\)

Mersenne primes and a proof by contrapositive

Numbers of the form \(2^n - 1\) are called Mersenne numbers, after the seventeenth-century French friar Marin Mersenne who catalogued them. When \(2^n - 1\) is prime we call it a Mersenne prime. As of 2024 every record-breaking largest known prime is a Mersenne prime, discovered by the distributed computing project GIMPS. The current record holder has over 24 million digits.

Before hunting for Mersenne primes there is a useful filter: if the exponent \(n\) is composite then \(2^n - 1\) is guaranteed to be composite too. This eliminates enormous swaths of candidates immediately.

The theorem

Theorem. If \(2^n - 1\) is prime, then \(n\) is prime.

The proof uses the contrapositive: instead of assuming \(2^n - 1\) is prime and deducing \(n\) is prime, we assume \(n\) is composite and deduce \(2^n - 1\) is composite. Suppose \(n = r \cdot s\) with \(r, s > 1\). Set \(x = 2^s\). Then the polynomial factorization \(x^r - 1 = (x - 1)(x^{r-1} + x^{r-2} + \cdots + x + 1)\) gives

\[ 2^n - 1 = x^r - 1 = (x - 1)\bigl(x^{r-1} + x^{r-2} + \cdots + x + 1\bigr). \]

Both factors exceed 1: since \(x = 2^s \geq 4\) we have \(x - 1 \geq 3\), and the second factor is a sum of \(r \geq 2\) positive terms. So \(2^n - 1\) is composite. \(\square\)

Note carefully: the converse is false. A prime exponent \(n\) does not guarantee \(2^n - 1\) is prime. The smallest counterexample is \(n = 11\): 11 is prime, yet \(2^{11} - 1 = 2047 = 23 \times 89\). Being prime is necessary for \(n\) but not sufficient.

Challenge problem

(a) Use the factorization above to write \(2^{15} - 1\) explicitly as a product of two factors, each greater than 1. (Note \(15 = 3 \times 5\).)

(b) Show by trial division that \(2^{11} - 1 = 2047\) is not prime. Find both prime factors.

(c) Among the exponents \(n = 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13\), which produce a Mersenne prime \(2^n - 1\)? Check each one. Is there a pattern in which prime exponents work?
