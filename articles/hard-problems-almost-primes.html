Almost Primes and Semiprimes

the building blocks one step above the primes

Not every integer is prime, but some are almost prime — they have very few prime factors. A number is called \(k\)-almost prime if it has exactly \(k\) prime factors counted with multiplicity. The 1-almost primes are the ordinary primes. The 2-almost primes, called semiprimes, include \(4 = 2^2\), \(6 = 2 \cdot 3\), \(9 = 3^2\), \(10 = 2 \cdot 5\), and so on.

Definition

Define \(\Omega(n)\) as the number of prime factors of \(n\) counted with multiplicity:

\[ \Omega(12) = \Omega(2^2 \cdot 3) = 3, \quad \Omega(30) = \Omega(2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5) = 3, \quad \Omega(p) = 1 \text{ for any prime } p. \]

A number is \(k\)-almost prime if and only if \(\Omega(n) = k\).

Why they matter

Almost primes are a surprisingly powerful tool for attacking hard problems. Consider the twin prime conjecture: infinitely many pairs \((p, p+2)\) are both prime. This remains unproven. But in 1973 Jing-Run Chen proved there are infinitely many pairs \((p, p+2)\) where \(p\) is prime and \(p + 2\) is either prime or semiprime — one step away from the full conjecture.

The Green-Tao theorem (2004) also passed through almost primes on its way to proving that the primes contain arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions. The key move was to embed the primes in a pseudorandom set of almost primes where density arguments could be applied. Almost primes are more abundant than primes, and their abundance gives mathematicians room to maneuver where the thinness of the primes makes direct attack impossible.

Challenge problem

(a) List all semiprimes between 1 and 50.

(b) Prove that for any semiprime \(n = p \cdot q\) with \(p \leq q\) we have \(p \leq \sqrt{n}\). Use this to design an algorithm for testing whether a given number is semiprime, and estimate how many trial divisions it needs in the worst case.

(c) Is \(n = 10403\) a semiprime? Apply your algorithm. (The answer is yes — find the two prime factors.)
