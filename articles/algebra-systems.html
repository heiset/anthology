Linear Systems and Inequalities

graphing, substitution, elimination, and regions

Solving Systems by Graphing

A system of linear equations is two or more equations that must all be true at the same time. The solution is the point \((x, y)\) that satisfies every equation — geometrically, the intersection of the lines. To check a candidate solution, substitute it into each equation and verify.

Checking a point. Is \((0, -4)\) a solution of the system \(-y - x = 4\) and \(y - 5 = -4\)?

\[ \begin{aligned} -y - x &= 4 && \Rightarrow\; -(-4) - 0 = 4 \;\checkmark \\[4pt] y - 5 &= -4 && \Rightarrow\; -4 - 5 = -9 \neq -4 \;\times \end{aligned} \]

The first equation holds but the second does not — \((0,-4)\) is not a solution.

Finding the intersection graphically. Solve the system \(5x - 4y = 8\) and \(-x + 2y = -4\).

Rewrite both in slope-intercept form: \(y = \tfrac{5}{4}x - 2\) and \(y = \tfrac{1}{2}x - 2\). Both pass through \((0, -2)\) — that's the intersection.

\[ \begin{aligned} 5(0) - 4(-2) &= 8 \;\checkmark \\ -(0) + 2(-2) &= -4 \;\checkmark \end{aligned} \qquad \boxed{(0,\,-2)} \]

System with a horizontal line. Solve \(3x - 5y = 2\) and \(y = 2\).

Rewrite the first equation: \(y = \tfrac{3}{5}x - \tfrac{2}{5}\). Graph this alongside \(y = 2\), then substitute \(y = 2\) into the first equation to find \(x\):

\[3x - 5(2) = 2 \;\Rightarrow\; 3x = 12 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 4\]
\[\boxed{(4,\,2)}\]

Solving Systems by Substitution

When one equation already has a variable isolated — or is easy to isolate — substitute that expression into the other equation. This collapses the system into a single equation with one unknown.

Solve: \(\;\begin{cases} y = 3x - 10 \\ 5x + 2y = -9 \end{cases}\)

Substitute \(y = 3x - 10\) into the second equation:

\[ \begin{aligned} 5x + 2(3x - 10) &= -9 \\ 5x + 6x - 20 &= -9 \\ 11x &= 11 \\ x &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

Back-substitute \(x = 1\) into \(y = 3x - 10\):

\[y = 3(1) - 10 = -7 \qquad \boxed{(1,\,-7)}\]

Solve: \(\;\begin{cases} 2x + 2y = 4 \\ y = 12 - 3x \end{cases}\)

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x + 2(12 - 3x) &= 4 \\ 2x + 24 - 6x &= 4 \\ -4x &= -20 \\ x &= 5 \end{aligned} \] \[y = 12 - 3(5) = -3 \qquad \boxed{(5,\,-3)}\]

Solve: \(\;\begin{cases} 4x + 3y = -1 \\ 3x + y = -7 \end{cases}\)

Neither equation has a variable isolated, but the second is easy to rearrange. Solve for \(y\):

\[y = -7 - 3x\]

Substitute into the first equation:

\[ \begin{aligned} 4x + 3(-7 - 3x) &= -1 \\ 4x - 21 - 9x &= -1 \\ -5x &= 20 \\ x &= -4 \end{aligned} \] \[y = -7 - 3(-4) = -7 + 12 = 5 \qquad \boxed{(-4,\,5)}\]

Solving Systems by Elimination

Elimination adds (or subtracts) the two equations to cancel one variable entirely. If the variable doesn't cancel naturally, multiply one or both equations first to create equal and opposite coefficients.

Solve: \(\;\begin{cases} 2x + y = 10 \\ 5x - y = 11 \end{cases}\)

The \(y\)-terms already have opposite signs — add the equations directly:

\[ \begin{aligned} (2x + y) + (5x - y) &= 10 + 11 \\ 7x &= 21 \\ x &= 3 \end{aligned} \] \[2(3) + y = 10 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 4 \qquad \boxed{(3,\,4)}\]

Solve: \(\;\begin{cases} 10x + 3y = 11 \\ 10x + 5y = 5 \end{cases}\)

The \(x\)-coefficients are equal, so multiply the first equation by \(-1\) to make them cancel:

\[ \begin{aligned} (-10x - 3y) + (10x + 5y) &= -11 + 5 \\ 2y &= -6 \\ y &= -3 \end{aligned} \] \[10x + 5(-3) = 5 \;\Rightarrow\; 10x = 20 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 2 \qquad \boxed{(2,\,-3)}\]

Solve: \(\;\begin{cases} 3x - 4y = 19 \\ 6x + 9y = 21 \end{cases}\)

Multiply the first equation by \(-2\) so the \(x\)-terms cancel:

\[ \begin{aligned} (-6x + 8y) + (6x + 9y) &= -38 + 21 \\ 17y &= -17 \\ y &= -1 \end{aligned} \] \[3x - 4(-1) = 19 \;\Rightarrow\; 3x + 4 = 19 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 5 \qquad \boxed{(5,\,-1)}\]

Graphing Linear Inequalities

A linear inequality like \(y < 2x + 2\) describes a region of the plane rather than a single line. The boundary line divides the plane in two; you then shade the half that makes the inequality true. Use a solid boundary for \(\le\) or \(\ge\) (the boundary is included) and a dashed boundary for \(<\) or \(>\) (it's not).

To check whether a point satisfies an inequality, substitute its coordinates and see if the statement holds.

Check \((-1, 2)\) in \(5x + 7y \le 10\).

\[5(-1) + 7(2) = -5 + 14 = 9 \le 10 \;\checkmark \qquad \text{is a solution}\]

Check \((3, -3)\) in \(-3x - 2y \ge 0\).

\[-3(3) - 2(-3) = -9 + 6 = -3 \ge 0? \quad \text{False} \qquad \text{not a solution}\]

Graph \(x \ge 4\).

The boundary is the vertical line \(x = 4\). Test \((0,0)\): \(0 \ge 4\) is false, so the origin is outside. Test \((5,0)\): \(5 \ge 4\) is true, so the region to the right is shaded.

Graph \(y < 2x + 2\).

Boundary \(y = 2x+2\) is dashed (strict inequality). Test \((0,0)\): \(0 < 2\) is true, so the region below the line is shaded. Test \((0,4)\): \(4 < 2\) is false.

Systems of Linear Inequalities

When you have two inequalities at once, the solution region is where both are satisfied — the overlap of the two shaded half-planes. Graph each inequality and shade; the solution is the area covered by both shadings.

Check \((2, 0)\) in the system \(y > x - 5\) and \(y \le 2x + 1\).

\[ \begin{aligned} 0 &> 2 - 5 = -3 &&\checkmark \\ 0 &\le 2(2) + 1 = 5 &&\checkmark \end{aligned} \]

Both hold — \((2,0)\) is a solution of the system.

Graph the system \(x + y \le 2\) and \(y \le 1\).

Test \((0,0)\): \(0+0 \le 2\) ✓ and \(0 \le 1\) ✓ — inside. Test \((3,1)\): \(3+1 \le 2\) ✗ — outside. The boundaries intersect at \((1, 1)\); shade the region below both lines.

Graph the system \(y \le \tfrac{1}{3}x + 2\) and \(y > -\tfrac{1}{2}x + 5\).

The solid line \(y = \tfrac{1}{3}x + 2\) (included) and dashed line \(y = -\tfrac{1}{2}x + 5\) (excluded) cross at \(\bigl(\tfrac{18}{5}, \tfrac{16}{5}\bigr)\). The solution region is the narrow wedge between the lines to the right of that crossing. Test \((6,3)\): \(3 \le 4\) ✓ and \(3 > 2\) ✓ — inside.

Writing a system from a graph. The graph below shows two dashed boundary lines with a shaded region between them.

To find the equations, identify two points on each line. Both lines pass through \((0, 3)\):

  • The steeper line also passes through \((1, 1)\): slope \(= \tfrac{1-3}{1-0} = -2\), so \(y = -2x + 3\).
  • The shallower line also passes through \((4, 4)\): slope \(= \tfrac{4-3}{4-0} = \tfrac{1}{4}\), so \(y = \tfrac{1}{4}x + 3\).

Now test the point \((4, 0)\) which appears to be in the shaded region:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{For } y = -2x+3: & \quad 0 \; ? \; -2(4)+3 = -5 && \Rightarrow\; 0 > -5 && \text{shaded side: } y > -2x+3 \\[4pt] \text{For } y = \tfrac{1}{4}x+3: & \quad 0 \; ? \; \tfrac{1}{4}(4)+3 = 4 && \Rightarrow\; 0 < 4 && \text{shaded side: } y < \tfrac{1}{4}x+3 \end{aligned} \] \[\boxed{\begin{cases} y > -2x + 3 \\[4pt] y < \tfrac{1}{4}x + 3 \end{cases}}\]
